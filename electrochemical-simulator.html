Electrochemical Energy — Guide
Section A
Electrochemical cells

Devices that convert chemical energy directly into electrical energy (or vice versa) via redox reactions at two electrodes separated by an electrolyte.

  • Anode (−) — oxidation occurs; electrode loses electrons to the external circuit
  • Cathode (+) — reduction occurs; electrode gains electrons from the external circuit
  • Electrolyte — ionic conductor (liquid or solid) that allows ion flow between electrodes while blocking electron flow
Section B
Types of cells
Galvanic cell
Spontaneous redox reaction produces electricity. ΔG < 0. Example: standard zinc–copper Daniell cell.
Electrolytic cell
External electricity drives a non-spontaneous reaction. ΔG > 0. Example: electrolysis of water, electroplating.
Section C
Batteries

A battery is a collection of one or more electrochemical cells packaged together to deliver usable electrical energy.

  • Primary (non-rechargeable) — reaction is irreversible; cell is discarded after use. Example: alkaline AA, zinc-carbon
  • Secondary (rechargeable) — reversible reaction; external current restores the cell. Example: lithium-ion (~250 Wh/kg), lead-acid (~40 Wh/kg), solid-state (~400 Wh/kg)
  • BMS (Battery Management System) — monitors voltage, temperature, and SOC; applies CC/CV charging and taper logic to protect cells
Section D
Fuel cells

Electrochemical devices that continuously convert hydrogen and oxygen into electricity, heat, and water — as long as fuel is supplied.

  • Anode reaction — 2H₂ → 4H⁺ + 4e⁻
  • Cathode reaction — O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂O
  • Ideal H₂ : O₂ ratio — 2 : 1 by mole; deviations reduce efficiency and power output
  • Efficiency — up to ~60–83% electrical efficiency; far cleaner than combustion engines (~25–35%)
  • PEM fuel cell — proton exchange membrane operates at 60–120 °C; peak efficiency near 80 °C
